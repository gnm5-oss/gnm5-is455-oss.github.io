Food Safety · Chicago · 2010–2025
Chicago Bakeries Didn't Get Dirtier in 2019. The Rulebook Just Got Stricter.
By Gayatri Mahindrakar & Harisankar Kartha · IS 445, Group 5
Between 2017 and 2019, the share of Chicago bakery inspections ending in a clean pass plummeted from roughly 70% to under 19%. Restaurants and grocery stores fell almost as far. The cause wasn't a citywide hygiene collapse — it was a single regulatory change that almost no one outside of the Chicago Department of Public Health noticed at the time.
Every year, the Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH) sends inspectors to tens of thousands of food establishments — restaurants, grocery stores, schools, bakeries, daycares, long-term-care facilities. Each visit ends in one of three outcomes: Pass, Pass with Conditions, or Fail. Across the 246,990 inspections we analyzed from 2010 through 2025, restaurants alone account for roughly 175,000 of them — nearly six times more than any other facility type.
For most of the 2010s, somewhere around two-thirds of those inspections came back as straight passes. Then 2018 happened.

The July 2018 Cliff
In July 2018, CDPH overhauled how its inspectors classify violations. Conditions that had previously been logged as minor were reclassified as "priority" or "priority foundation" issues — the kind that, under the new framework, could push an inspection from Pass into Pass with Conditions or even Fail. Nothing about the underlying cleanliness of Chicago kitchens changed overnight. The yardstick did.
The result, visible in nearly every panel above, is a synchronized collapse of pass rates between 2017 and 2019. The chart below makes the size of the drop concrete.
Each row compares one facility type's pass rate in 2017 (green dot) to 2019 (red dot). Bakeries lost the most ground — over 48 percentage points in two years. Schools, which face the strictest baseline scrutiny year-round, fell the least.
Different Sectors, Different Recoveries
By 2025, most facility types had clawed back to roughly where they sat before the rule change — restaurants and bakeries hovering in the low 60s, childcare facilities in the mid-70s, schools and long-term-care somewhere in between. What's striking is that the pre-2018 hierarchy reappeared almost intact: the sectors that ran high before the cliff (Children's Services Facilities, the two daycare categories) are running high again, and the sectors that ran lower (Restaurants, Grocery Stores, Long Term Care) are still lower. The cliff was universal, but the recovery wasn't a level-up — it was a return to baseline.
A second, smaller dip in 2020 cuts across every facility type. That one's easier to explain: COVID-19 collapsed the volume of in-person inspections, and the inspections that did happen tended to focus on the most at-risk establishments. Both effects show up clearly in the heatmap below.
Each cell shows the pass rate for one facility type in one year. The 2018–2019 column is a wall of red across nearly every row — a structural change, not a behavioral one. The gradual recovery from left to right after 2020 shows facilities adapting to the stricter framework.
What Happens on Day One?
The charts above track every inspection over 15 years. But what about a food business's very first visit from an inspector? The first inspection is often the most consequential — it shapes an owner's perception of the regulatory system and sets the baseline for their operating standards. By linking inspections to business licenses issued by the City of Chicago, we can isolate each establishment's inaugural inspection and ask: how many new food businesses pass on their first try?
The answer, across all categories, is roughly half. Of every 100 new Chicago food businesses that open their doors and face their first CDPH inspection, about 54 pass cleanly, 12 pass with conditions attached, and 34 fail outright. One in three new food businesses fails its very first health inspection.
The breakdown varies sharply by license type. Mobile food vendors — food trucks and carts — fare better, with 59 out of 100 passing cleanly. Wholesale food establishments tell the opposite story: only 48 out of 100 pass, while 51 fail — a majority of new wholesale businesses can't clear the bar on their first attempt.
Each bar shows the share of first-ever inspections ending in Pass (green), Pass with Conditions (amber), or Fail (red) for each license category. The more red, the harder that category finds it to pass on day one.
The Inspection Blind Spots
Pass rates tell you how scored inspections turn out. They say nothing about the establishments that never get scored at all. To find those blind spots, we compared every food-related business license the city has issued against CDPH's actual inspection records. For licenses that have been active for at least two years — long enough that the city has had ample opportunity to send an inspector — we asked a simple question: has this business ever been inspected?
For Retail Food establishments — the restaurants, grocery stores, and bakeries that most Chicagoans interact with daily — the answer is reassuring: only 114 out of 9,213 long-active licenses (about 1.2%) have never seen an inspector. That's a testament to CDPH's focus on the highest-traffic food businesses.
But the picture changes dramatically in other categories. Among wholesale food businesses, 301 out of 347 active licenses — nearly 87% — have never received a single inspection. Shared kitchen supplemental licenses are even worse: 73 of 81 (90%) have never been inspected. These aren't consumer-facing storefronts, but they sit in the supply chain that feeds Chicago's restaurant economy, and they operate under the same licensing framework.
Each bar shows the share of long-active food licenses (held for 2+ years) that have never received a CDPH inspection. Retail food is well-covered at 1.2%, but wholesale and supplemental kitchen categories have massive gaps — the vast majority have never been inspected.
Where in Chicago Are the Gaps?
Even within Retail Food — the category where CDPH's coverage is strongest — the 114 uninspected businesses aren't scattered evenly. The map below places every long-active retail food license that has never been inspected as a dot on Chicago's ZIP code map, with each ZIP shaded by the share of its active licenses that slipped through.
The pattern reveals geographic clustering. ZIPs in parts of the West and South sides — including 60608 (Pilsen/Bridgeport, 11 uninspected out of 276 licenses), 60639 (Belmont Cragin, 10 of 278), and 60619 (Chatham/Avalon Park, 7 of 161) — show the highest uninspected rates, consistently above 3.5%. Most North Side ZIPs, by contrast, sit below 1%. The gaps are small in absolute terms, but they are not random — they track with the same neighborhoods that tend to have fewer city resources directed toward code enforcement more broadly.

What This Means for Chicago Diners
A reader scanning a 2024 inspection report and a 2014 report side-by-side could be forgiven for thinking Chicago's food scene got measurably worse. It didn't — at least not by the underlying measure. What changed is the definition of "good enough." That distinction matters: pass-rate trends are increasingly used by journalists, ratings sites, and even insurance underwriters as a proxy for food safety. When the definition shifts under everyone's feet without a corresponding public communication campaign, the data tells a story the city didn't intend to tell.
Meanwhile, the coverage story adds a second wrinkle. CDPH does an admirable job reaching the city's restaurants and grocery stores — fewer than 2% of long-active retail food licenses have gone uninspected. But entire categories of the food supply chain — wholesale distributors, supplemental kitchens — operate for years without any oversight. And even within the well-covered retail category, the gaps aren't evenly distributed: they cluster in neighborhoods on the West and South sides that have historically received less public investment.
The good news: even under the stricter post-2018 framework, the majority of Chicago's food establishments still pass. The not-as-good news: where you eat — and what kind of business prepares your food — still predicts how closely the city is watching.
Methodology & Sources
Primary dataset
Chicago Food Inspections. City of Chicago Open Data Portal, maintained by the Chicago Department of Public Health. data.cityofchicago.org. Accessed 2026. 309,344 raw records spanning January 2010 through April 2026; filtered to 246,990 records with valid Pass/Fail/Pass-w-Conditions outcomes, valid risk levels, and complete years (2010–2025), restricted to the 8 most-inspected facility types.
Contextual dataset
Chicago Business Licenses. City of Chicago Open Data Portal. data.cityofchicago.org. Filtered server-side via the Socrata API to 14 food-related license categories. Used to compute first-inspection outcomes, identify long-active licenses never inspected, and map uninspected retail food businesses by ZIP code.
Geographic boundaries
Chicago ZIP Code Boundaries. GeoJSON boundary file sourced from the City of Chicago, hosted via lseemann/Chicago_ZIP_Codes on GitHub.
Regulatory background
Chicago Department of Public Health, Food Protection Division. Public announcement of revised violation classification framework, July 2018.

The full filtering, aggregation, and chart-generation pipeline is available as a Jupyter notebook: FP_Part2_IS445.ipynb (also viewable directly on GitHub).
